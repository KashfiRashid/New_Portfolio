## The anchor

Kash's voice is **Tazwar Tarik–adjacent, but more direct**. Warm. Reflective at the right moments, declarative most of the time. Confident without being loud. Sets up → hits → lands. Doesn't linger after the metaphor.

This is calibrated against Kash's actual LinkedIn posts (in `/refs/voice/`). If voice ever drifts back toward over-hedging or literary linger, re-read those posts. The cadence to match is:

- *"We lost so bad. Oh wait. April Fools. We won."* — setup → reversal → land. No softening.
- *"People told he was lost. But we saw focus."* — declarative metaphor in two sentences. Doesn't elaborate.
- *"Ideas were pouring in, but we lacked structure. I stepped in, assigned roles."* — direct ownership of action. No "I'm not sure when that happened."
- *"watching people actually play Something Lurking... that was the summit."* — specific physical moment named as the climax.
- *"Simple idea but it was my first time connecting text generation to procedural visuals and seeing it come together was pretty cool."* — short reflective close. One sentence, then done.

The previous voice file leaned too hard on *"turns back on itself"* and *"honest about uncertainty."* Both are real patterns in Kash's voice, but they're **rare moves**, not constant ones. Use them once per piece, max. The default mode is direct.

## Voice characteristics (reordered)

### 1. Direct first, warm always

The voice doesn't punch declaratives at people *(that's corporate)*. It also doesn't hedge into uncertainty *(that's literary fiction)*. It says specific true things, plainly, and trusts the reader to catch them.

- *"Most of what I do happens at 2am. that's not a brag, it's just where the focus shows up."* — direct claim, direct disclaimer, done
- *"I think about users the way I think about friends — by name."* — specific image, lands fast
- *"Restraint reads as confidence. quieter than the rest, on purpose."* — declarative principle, no hedging follow-up

Compare to what we're rejecting:

- *"I'm trying to write a portfolio that earns the right to whisper."* — too literary, "trying to" hedges, the "earns the right" is over-poetic for the register
- *"shows up in the work in ways I'm still mapping"* — the mapping language adds nothing; *"shows up in the work everywhere"* lands harder

### 2. Set up → hit → land (don't linger)

Kash's actual writing pattern when a metaphor or moment shows up: he sets it up briefly, hits the point, and stops. He does **not** elaborate the metaphor for three more sentences. This is the single most important pattern to get right.

- *"People told he was lost. But we saw focus. And that is what we took as a team."* — three sentences, total. No exegesis on what focus means.
- *"watching people actually play Something Lurking... that was the summit."* — names the moment, calls it the summit, moves on.

If you write a metaphor and then explain it, cut the explanation. The point IS the metaphor.

### 3. Specific images, named people

*"My friend Sazzad in Dhaka, who I wrote a refugee letter for"* beats *"a friend back home"* every time.

- *"Sadab, Tawheed, Brett — the three I keep showing up to hackathons with"* > *"my hackathon team"*
- *"the IT Squad at FIC, where my Tuesday afternoons disappear into incident triage"* > *"my IT job"*

If you find yourself writing an abstraction, stop and ask whether there's a named-person version of the same sentence. There usually is.

### 4. Lowercase casual where it fits — not as a tic

Lowercase for asides, hot takes, and companion bubbles. Sentence case for declarations, bios, and section openers. Mixing both is what makes it feel real instead of stylized.

- *"i plan things to death before i touch the keyboard. it's a problem and a feature."* — companion bubble, lowercase fits
- *"Kashfi Rashid. Designer, developer, sound person, son. Mostly the same person in each."* — bio, sentence case fits

### 5. One hedge per piece, max

The voice can say *"I'm still figuring this out"* — but **once**, not as a constant tic. Most claims should land confidently. Hedging earns its place by being rare.

- ✓ Use a hedge for genuinely unresolved things (*"I'm still figuring out what this site is"*)
- ✗ Don't hedge facts you actually know (*"...in ways I'm still mapping"* when you know exactly how it shows up)

### 6. Doesn't oversell

If a project went well, say so plainly. If it was messy, say that. Both register as honesty, which is what makes the wins land.

- *"BLU's the proudest I've been of any school project. specifically the sound — the visuals had real holes."* — both halves coexist
- *"it ran cleaner than it should have."* — the *should have* admits the rough conditions, the *cleaner* still claims the win

## How Kash speaks about his team (new — surfaced from LinkedIn)

A pattern worth flagging: **Kash names every collaborator with role attribution.** This shows up consistently in his real writing and should be the People section's discipline.

Examples from his real posts:

- *"Eric Gabriel Cheng Li as our sole 3D Modeler, building the entire world from the control rooms down to the microchips."*
- *"Kentaro Weil and Michael Kim led the Mechanics, engineering the complex socket interactors and physics systems that made the ship function."*
- *"Sadab, Tawheed, Brett, and me, mostly between midnight and 4am."*
- *"And to Hamzah Alshawwaf, thank you for being an amazing mentor. Genuinely made a difference."*

The pattern: **name + role + specific contribution.** Not just *"thanks team."* People page bios should follow this template.

Also worth flagging: when leadership was needed, Kash claims it directly, not euphemistically. *"I stepped in, assigned roles, and we worked hard to set the foundation."* Not *"I helped facilitate"* or *"I supported the team direction."* The directness here is part of the voice — own what you did, plainly.

## Anti-patterns — the dead-on-arrival list

Every line below appears on tens of thousands of portfolios in 2026. None of them appear on this site. Not in modified form. Not in disguise. **If you catch yourself writing one of these or its sibling, stop and rewrite.**

- *"Passionate about creating delightful user experiences."*
- *"I'm a multidisciplinary designer who blends design and technology."*
- *"Driven by curiosity and a love for clean, intentional design."*
- *"Bridging the gap between..."*
- *"Empowering teams to..."*
- *"From pixels to pull requests, I keep ideas moving."* (this is on the *current* site — kill it)
- *"Designing is collaborative — let's start with a chat."* (current site — kill it)
- *"As a [role], I believe..."*
- *"I bring a unique perspective to..."*
- *"My approach is rooted in..."*
- *"Crafting intuitive, scalable, human-centered experiences."*
- *"Earns the right to whisper."* — *(from voice v1; pulled in v1.1 for being too literary)*
- Any sentence with three adjectives in a row.
- Any sentence with the word *"holistic"* in it.
- Any sentence that lingers on its own metaphor for more than two sentences.
- Any sentence that could appear on ten thousand other portfolios without changing a word.

## Sample misdirect openers (new — surfaced from LinkedIn)

Kash's real writing uses **misdirect openers** — a sentence that seems to set up one thing, immediately reverses, lands. This is a usable pattern for Hall of Fame entries, Process closers, project recap moments, anywhere a small jolt fits.

Examples from his actual posts:

- *"We lost so bad. Oh wait. April Fools. We won."*
- *"People told he was lost. But we saw focus."*

Adaptable templates:

- *"This was supposed to be small. it wasn't."*
- *"Three of us thought it would take a week. it took a month. it shipped."*
- *"I almost didn't enter. that would've been the wrong call."*

Use sparingly. One per page max. The pattern loses force if it's the default.

- *"...you skipped the part about me. fair, I made it short."*
- *"breezing through. that's allowed."*

### Idle (12s of no movement)
- *"want to see what I was working on at 2am last Tuesday?"*
- *"there's a clip in here that's just my desk. I think you'd like it."*

### Exit-intent (cursor heading for the X)
- *"leaving? cool. tell a friend, or don't."*
- *"the hall of fame's down at the bottom if you have a second."*

### Returning visitor
- *"hey [name], your color's still red. sit anywhere."*
- *"you're back. the site's a little different than last time."*

> Note on tone: none of these are eager. None of them try to keep the visitor on the site. The companion is observational and quiet. That restraint is what makes it not feel like Clippy.

## Voice cheat sheet (updated)

Pin this in any handoff doc.

### Six do's *(was 5 — added "land your metaphors fast")*
1. **Use named people.** *"Sazzad,"* *"Sadab,"* *"the IT Squad,"* not *"a friend,"* *"a teammate,"* *"my coworkers."*
2. **Lowercase casual where it fits.** Asides, companion bubbles, hot takes get lowercase. Bios, declarations, section openers stay sentence case.
3. **Land your metaphors fast.** Set up → hit → land in three sentences max. If you elaborate the metaphor, cut the elaboration. The point IS the metaphor. ← new in v1.1
4. **Be honest about uncertainty — once.** *"I'm still figuring this out"* is allowed and good, but use it sparingly. One hedge per piece, not a tic.
5. **Pin specific moments.** A Tuesday in March, a take, a 0:42 timestamp, a 9-year friendship — concrete beats abstract every time.
6. **Name the team with role and contribution.** *"Eric, our sole 3D Modeler, who built the world from control rooms to microchips"* — not *"thanks team."*

### Five don'ts
1. **Don't write the anti-patterns above.** Re-check against the list before publishing anything.
2. **Don't sand down "ambitious but executioneery."** It is not a typo. It is the brand.
3. **Don't perform humility or perform brilliance.** Both read as performance. Just notice things and say them.
4. **Don't lead with role or stack.** *"UX/UI Designer at..."* / *"React, Tailwind, framer-motion..."* — lead with what's interesting first; tools come later if at all.
5. **Don't oversell.** If a project had holes, say it had holes. The honesty is what makes the wins land.

*Specifics over adjectives. People over abstractions. Honesty over polish. Direct over hedged. Lowercase where it fits.*
